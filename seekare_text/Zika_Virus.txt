intro
Zika virus is a mosquito-borne flavivirus primarily transmitted by the Aedes species of mosquitoes, particularly Aedes aegypti and Aedes albopictus. Initially identified in Uganda's Zika Forest in 1947, it was considered a relatively mild infection with limited impact on human health until the significant outbreaks in the Pacific and the Americas in the 2010s, which revealed its potential to cause severe congenital anomalies. The virus can also be transmitted through sexual contact, blood transfusion, and from mother to fetus during pregnancy. Infection during pregnancy is notably associated with birth defects such as microcephaly and other congenital abnormalities known collectively as congenital Zika syndrome. While most infections are asymptomatic or result in only mild symptoms like rash, fever, joint pain, and conjunctivitis, the potential implications for pregnant women and global public health have led to significant research and prevention efforts.

subsection:Signs and Symptoms
subtitle:What are the common signs and symptoms of Zika?
The majority of people infected with Zika virus do not develop symptoms. In cases where symptoms do manifest, they are usually mild and last for several days to a week. Typical signs and symptoms of Zika virus infection can include:

1. **Fever**: Often low-grade, it is not necessarily present in all cases.
2. **Rash**: This can be a flat, red area on the skin that is covered with small bumps.
3. **Joint Pain**: Typically observed in the hands and feet, the pain can sometimes be quite severe.
4. **Conjunctivitis**: Also known as red or pink eye, it is characterized by inflammation or infection of the outer membrane of the eyeball and the inner eyelid.
5. **Muscle Pain**: Myalgia is common and can be widespread.
6. **Headache**: This can vary in intensity.
7. **Malaise**: A general feeling of discomfort, unease, or lack of well-being.

Symptoms usually last from several days to a week, and hospitalization is rare. The Zika virus infection during pregnancy can cause infants to be born with a congenital condition known as microcephaly and other fetal brain defects. Additionally, there have been increased reports of Guillain-Barré syndrome, an uncommon sickness of the nervous system, in areas affected by Zika.

subsection: Types of Zika virus
subtitle:Are there different types of Zika viruses?
Zika virus is a single type of virus that belongs to the Flavivirus genus, which also includes other viruses such as dengue, yellow fever, and West Nile virus. However, when discussing "types" in the context of Zika, one could be referring to the strains and lineages of the virus or to the modes of transmission.

**Strains and Lineages:**
There are two main lineages of the Zika virus:

1. **African lineage:** The original strain of Zika virus was first identified in monkeys in the Zika forest of Uganda in 1947, and subsequently in humans in 1952. This lineage is considered to be endemic in some parts of Africa.
2. **Asian lineage:** This lineage includes the strain that has spread to the Pacific islands and the Americas, causing the large outbreaks seen in recent years. The Asian lineage is associated with the significant outbreaks in Micronesia, French Polynesia, and Brazil, which began in 2007, 2013, and 2015, respectively.

**Modes of Transmission:**
While there's only one Zika virus, it can be transmitted in several ways:

1. **Vector-borne transmission:** The primary mode of transmission is through the bite of an infected Aedes species mosquito, specifically Aedes aegypti and Aedes albopictus.
2. **Sexual transmission:** Zika can be passed through sex, even if the infected person does not have symptoms at the time.
3. **From mother to child:** A pregnant woman can pass Zika virus to her fetus during pregnancy or around the time of birth.
4. **Through blood transfusion:** There have been reported cases of the Zika virus being transmitted through blood transfusions.

There have been no significant mutations reported that have led to different types of Zika virus with differing effects on humans; the major concerns with Zika virus have been its spread and its impact on pregnant women and their fetuses.

subsection: Causes of Zika virus
subtitle:What causes Zika infections?
Zika virus is primarily caused by the Zika virus itself, which is a member of the virus family Flaviviridae and the genus Flavivirus, but the infection can be attributed to several factors related to the transmission of this virus. Here are the main causes or modes of transmission:

1. **Mosquito Bites:**
   - The virus is mainly transmitted to humans through the bites of infected female mosquitoes, primarily the Aedes aegypti and Aedes albopictus species. These mosquitoes usually bite during the day, especially around dawn and dusk.

2. **Sexual Transmission:**
   - Zika virus can be spread through sexual intercourse. This includes vaginal, anal, and oral sex, as well as the sharing of sex toys with a person who is infected with Zika.

3. **Maternal-Fetal Transmission:**
   - Pregnant women infected with Zika virus can transmit the virus to their fetus during pregnancy or around the time of birth, leading to serious birth defects like microcephaly and other congenital Zika syndrome features.

4. **Blood Transfusion:**
   - There have been confirmed cases of Zika virus transmission through blood transfusions.

5. **Laboratory Exposure:**
   - There have also been documented cases where the virus was transmitted in a laboratory setting due to accidental exposure.

6. **Organ or Tissue Transplantation:**
   - There is a potential risk of Zika virus transmission through organ or tissue transplants from an infected donor, although actual cases are extremely rare.

7. **Travel to Endemic Areas:**
   - Travelling to or living in areas where the Zika virus is endemic or where outbreaks are occurring increases the risk of becoming infected with the virus.

The virus itself is not caused by these activities, but these are the mechanisms by which the virus can spread and cause infection in humans. Preventive measures focus on controlling mosquito populations, practicing safe sex, especially in areas where Zika is prevalent, and implementing screening protocols for blood transfusions and organ transplants.

subsection: Findings on Laboratory Studies
subtitle:How does Zika show up on labs
In patients with Zika virus infection, laboratory findings can vary and often require specific tests to confirm the presence of the virus, as the infection can be asymptomatic or present with nonspecific flu-like symptoms. Here are some of the expected laboratory findings and diagnostic tests used for Zika virus:

1. **RT-PCR (Reverse Transcription Polymerase Chain Reaction):**
   - This test detects the genetic material of the Zika virus and is most effective when performed within the first week of symptom onset, as the viremia is typically highest during the acute phase of infection.

2. **Serology Tests:**
   - These tests look for antibodies that the body produces in response to the virus. There are two main types:
     - **IgM antibodies:** which typically develop within a few days of infection and can be indicative of a recent infection. However, they may persist for up to 12 weeks, which can complicate the timing of infection.
     - **IgG antibodies:** which usually develop later and can last for years. However, due to cross-reactivity with other flaviviruses like dengue or West Nile virus, the presence of IgG antibodies can sometimes be difficult to interpret.

3. **Plaque Reduction Neutralization Test (PRNT):**
   - This more specific test can help differentiate between cross-reacting antibodies from other flavivirus infections. It is often used to confirm positive or equivocal serology results.

4. **Complete Blood Count (CBC):**
   - While not specific to Zika, a CBC might show mild leukopenia (reduced white blood cell count), thrombocytopenia (low platelet count), or anemia in some patients.

5. **Urine Tests:**
   - Zika virus RNA can often be detected in urine for a longer period than in blood and can be useful for diagnosis when the blood test is negative but suspicion remains high.

In pregnant women suspected of having Zika virus infection, ultrasound may be used to check for fetal abnormalities, such as microcephaly or other signs consistent with congenital Zika syndrome.

Due to the potential for cross-reactivity with other viruses and the limitations in the timing of testing, it is recommended that laboratory testing for Zika virus be guided by clinical and epidemiological factors and performed in consultation with public health officials.

subsection:Findings on Imaging Studies
subtitle:How does Zika show up on imaging studies?
Imaging studies are not typically used for the direct diagnosis of Zika virus infection in the infected individual. However, imaging studies play a crucial role when assessing the impact of Zika virus infection on fetal development during pregnancy and in the investigation of Zika virus-associated complications.

Here are some findings that might be seen on imaging studies in these contexts:

1. **Prenatal Ultrasound:**
   - In fetuses, ultrasound can detect microcephaly (a significantly smaller head than expected, often due to abnormal brain development) and intracranial calcifications.
   - Findings may also include ventriculomegaly (enlargement of the ventricles in the brain), decreased brain tissue with a specific pattern of brain damage that includes subcortical calcifications, and abnormalities of the corpus callosum.
   - Other possible findings include abnormal eye development, arthrogryposis (joint contractures), and hydrops fetalis (excessive fluid accumulation in fetal compartments).

2. **MRI (Magnetic Resonance Imaging):**
   - For infants and fetuses, MRI can provide more detailed images of the brain structure and may reveal a pattern of widespread brain tissue loss and associated white matter hypointensities, delayed myelination, brainstem hypoplasia, and a reduction in white matter volume.
   - Postnatal MRI of the brain can be used to assess the degree of cortical malformations, cerebellar anomalies, and the extent of damage to the brain structure.

3. **CT (Computed Tomography) Scan:**
   - A CT scan might show calcifications in the brain, which are suggestive but not specific to congenital Zika syndrome.
   - It can also detect microcephaly and other structural abnormalities in the brain.

Imaging studies are not used to diagnose Zika virus infection in the general population, but they are integral to the management of Zika virus infection during pregnancy and the assessment of Zika-related congenital abnormalities. The findings on imaging in infants and fetuses can often be quite severe and lead to lifelong disability or developmental delays. These imaging studies assist in planning the management of the pregnancy and the care of the child after birth.

subsection: Diagnosis
subtitle:How is Zika diagnosed?
The diagnosis of Zika virus infection can be challenging because its symptoms overlap with many other arboviral diseases such as dengue and chikungunya, and many infections are asymptomatic. However, when the diagnosis is pursued, it involves a combination of clinical evaluation, epidemiological information, and laboratory tests.

**Clinical Evaluation:**
- A thorough clinical assessment is necessary, which includes taking a medical history to check for common symptoms of Zika virus infection (fever, rash, joint pain, conjunctivitis) and understanding recent travel history to areas with known outbreaks.

**Epidemiological Information:**
- Travel history to areas with known Zika transmission or unprotected sex with a partner who has traveled to these areas can raise suspicion for Zika infection.

**Laboratory Tests:**
- **Molecular testing (RT-PCR):** For people with symptoms, the CDC recommends testing serum or urine samples using reverse transcription-polymerase chain reaction (RT-PCR) to detect viral RNA. This test is most reliable when done during the first week after symptom onset.
- **Serology:** Antibody testing can detect the presence of IgM and neutralizing antibodies against Zika virus. However, serological tests can cross-react with other flaviviruses like dengue and West Nile virus, which can complicate the interpretation of results.
- **Plaque Reduction Neutralization Test (PRNT):** This can help discern between cross-reacting antibodies in people previously exposed to or vaccinated against other flaviviruses. This test measures the ability of antibodies to neutralize Zika virus and can be used to confirm initial IgM testing results.

For pregnant women, the CDC has specific guidelines for testing, which include Zika virus RNA nucleic acid testing (NAT) and IgM ELISA testing both initially and follow-up testing after 2-12 weeks following symptom onset or last possible exposure.

**Confirmation of Infection:**
- A positive RT-PCR result confirms Zika virus infection.
- In the case of a positive or equivocal IgM result with negative RT-PCR (if done within 7 days of symptom onset), PRNT may be performed to confirm the diagnosis due to potential cross-reactivity with related flaviviruses.

Because of the complexities of testing and the potential for false positives or false negatives, healthcare providers often work closely with local and state health departments to ensure proper testing and interpretation of results. It's important to note that testing recommendations may evolve as more is understood about the virus and its spread.

subsection: Treatment
subtitle:How is Zika treated?
There is no specific treatment or vaccine currently available for Zika virus infection. Management of Zika virus is supportive and symptomatic, meaning that it aims to relieve the symptoms rather than cure the infection itself. Here are the general supportive treatment measures that are recommended:

1. **Rest:** Patients are advised to get plenty of rest to help their bodies fight off the virus.

2. **Hydration:** Adequate fluid intake is important to prevent dehydration, especially in the presence of fever and sweating.

3. **Antipyretics and Pain Relievers:** Medications such as acetaminophen (Tylenol) or paracetamol can be used to relieve fever and pain. It's important to avoid aspirin and other nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) like ibuprofen until dengue can be ruled out, to reduce the risk of hemorrhage.

4. **Avoidance of Mosquito Bites:** During the first week of infection, Zika virus can be found in the blood and passed from an infected person to a mosquito through mosquito bites. Preventing mosquito bites during this time can help stop the spread of the virus.

For pregnant women infected with Zika virus, specialized prenatal care is crucial to monitor fetal development and manage any complications that may arise.

At present, there are no antiviral medications that are used or recommended for the treatment of Zika virus infection. Research is ongoing into developing a vaccine and specific antiviral treatments for Zika virus. If a patient is taking medicine for another medical condition, they should discuss with their healthcare provider whether they should continue to take their medicine as they normally would if they contract Zika virus.

Public health measures are also important for prevention, including vector control strategies to reduce mosquito populations, public education to avoid mosquito bites, and travel advisories for regions where Zika virus is circulating.

subsection: Prognosis
subtitle:What is the prognosis for patients with Zika infection?
The prognosis for most individuals infected with the Zika virus is generally good. The majority of Zika virus infections are mild and self-limited, and most people recover fully without any serious complications. However, there are certain populations and circumstances where the prognosis can vary:

1. **Mild Cases:** Most Zika virus infections cause mild symptoms such as fever, rash, joint pain, and conjunctivitis, which typically resolve within a week or two.

2. **Asymptomatic Cases:** Many people infected with Zika virus won’t experience any symptoms at all and may not even realize they've been infected. These cases also have a good prognosis, as there is no illness to resolve.

3. **Pregnancy:** The prognosis becomes more concerning when a pregnant woman contracts Zika virus, as it can lead to serious birth defects, including microcephaly and other congenital Zika syndrome characteristics. The extent of these effects can vary widely, with some being more severe than others.

4. **Neurological Complications:** Though rare, there can be severe neurological complications in some individuals, such as Guillain-Barré syndrome, neuropathy, and myelitis. These conditions can have varying outcomes, from complete recovery to long-term disability, depending on the severity and response to treatment.

5. **Chronic Symptoms:** A small number of individuals may report prolonged symptoms such as fatigue and joint pain, but this is not well-characterized and the long-term prognosis is still being studied.

The long-term outlook for adults and children who have had Zika virus infection and did not develop neurological complications is generally considered favorable. Ongoing research aims to understand the full spectrum of potential health effects and the possible long-term outcomes for affected individuals. Public health efforts continue to focus on prevention, particularly for pregnant women and those planning to become pregnant in areas where Zika is known to be present.
